History & Culture

For thousands of years, Native peoples lived in what is now Washington, relying on salmon, cedar trees, and the rich natural environment. They built longhouses, carved canoes, and passed down knowledge through stories and songs.

In the 1850s, the U.S. government signed treaties with tribes, promising fishing and hunting rights but taking much of their land. Leaders like Chief Leschi resisted unfair treatment and are honored today.

Despite hardships like disease, boarding schools, and broken treaties, Washington’s tribes continue to preserve their cultures through powwows, language programs, and community traditions.

Timeline of Native History in Washington

  • Before 1700s: Native peoples thrive for thousands of years, living in longhouses, fishing salmon, and building strong trade networks.
  • 1770s: Contact with explorers brings trade goods but also devastating new diseases.
  • 1854–1855: Treaties like the Treaty of Point Elliott and Treaty of Medicine Creek are signed. Tribes keep fishing rights but lose much land.
  • 1855–1858: Leaders like Chief Leschi of the Nisqually resist unfair treaties. The Yakama War and conflicts in eastern Washington erupt.
  • Late 1800s: Boarding schools try to erase Native languages and traditions.
  • 1900s: Tribes fight to keep fishing rights as dams and restrictions limit salmon runs.
  • 1974: The Boldt Decision reaffirms tribal treaty fishing rights, making tribes co-managers of fisheries.
  • 1990s–2000s: Tribes strengthen economies and revive languages and traditions.
  • Today: Washington’s 29 tribes continue to protect salmon, teach traditions, and govern as sovereign nations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How many tribes are in Washington?
A: There are 29 federally recognized tribes.

Q: Why are salmon so important?
A: Salmon are central to food, culture, and spiritual traditions. Treaties protect tribal fishing rights.

Q: Do tribes still speak Native languages?
A: Yes, many tribes are reviving their languages through schools and programs.

Q: What does sovereignty mean?
A: It means tribes govern themselves and manage their own communities.

Q: What is a reservation?
A: A reservation is land set aside for a tribe through treaties or agreements.

Q: What is a powwow?
A: A powwow is a Native gathering with dance, music, food, and celebration.

Q: Who was Chief Leschi?
A: A Nisqually leader who defended his people’s land in the 1850s, remembered as a hero.
