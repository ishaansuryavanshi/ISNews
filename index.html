History & Culture

For thousands of years, the tribes of Washington thrived by living in harmony with the land and waters. Salmon, cedar, and the rich forests shaped their daily lives. Tribes built longhouses, carved canoes, and passed down knowledge orally through stories, songs, and ceremonies.

European contact in the 1700s introduced trade, disease, and upheaval. In the 1850s, treaties like the Treaty of Point Elliott and Treaty of Medicine Creek often took lands while promising fishing rights. Leaders like Chief Leschi of the Nisqually resisted unjust treaties.

In the late 1800s, boarding schools attempted to erase Native languages and traditions, yet tribes preserved their culture through powwows, ceremonies, and storytelling. Today, tribes operate cultural centers, language programs, and environmental initiatives, blending tradition with modern governance.

Tribal languages include Lushootseed, Sahaptin, Salish, Klallam, Makah, and many more. Storytelling remains central, including creation myths, animal legends, and histories of resilience.

Sacred Landmarks

Snoqualmie Falls

Height 268 feet; sacred to Snoqualmie people, represents life, healing, and spiritual connection.

Mount Rainier (Tahoma)

Central to Coast Salish origin stories; site of spiritual vision quests and ceremonies.

Columbia River

Vital for salmon fishing and trade; integral to multiple tribes’ economies and culture.

Hoh Rainforest

Stewarded by Hoh Tribe; home to ancient cedar groves and salmon-bearing rivers.

Cape Flattery

Makah lands; location of whaling traditions and ceremonial sites.

Timeline of Native History in Washington

  • Before 1700s: Tribes live in longhouses, fish salmon, hunt, gather, and develop trade networks.
  • 1700–1770s: Explorers arrive; trade goods exchanged; diseases devastate populations.
  • 1854–1855: Treaties signed (Point Elliott, Medicine Creek); land ceded but fishing rights retained.
  • 1855–1858: Yakama War and conflicts; leaders like Chief Leschi resist unfair treaties.
  • Late 1800s: Boarding schools suppress Native languages; tribes fight to preserve culture.
  • 1900s: Salmon runs threatened by dams; tribes advocate for fishing rights.
  • 1974: Boldt Decision affirms treaty fishing rights; tribes co-manage fisheries.
  • 1990s–2000s: Tribes revive languages, culture, and economic initiatives.
  • Today: Washington’s 29 tribes protect salmon, teach traditions, and govern sovereign communities.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How many tribes are in Washington?
A: 29 federally recognized tribes.

Q: Why are salmon so important?
A: Central to food, culture, and spiritual life; treaties protect fishing rights.

Q: Do tribes still speak Native languages?
A: Yes, many languages are revived via schools and programs.

Q: What does sovereignty mean?
A: Tribes govern themselves and manage their communities.

Q: What is a powwow?
A: A gathering with dance, music, food, and celebration of culture.

Q: Who was Chief Leschi?
A: Nisqually leader who defended his people’s land in the 1850s.
